1. Introduction: What is a Generative Model?

Imagine you have a large collection of handwritten digits. A generative model learns the underlying patterns so well that it can produce brand new, realistic samples that look like they belong in your collection, even though they were never seen before.

There are three major families of generative models, each with different trade-offs:

Model TypeHow it worksTrade-off
GANsA generator and discriminator compete against each otherSharp samples, but training is unstable
VAEsEncode to latent space, decode back to dataStable training, but blurry samples
Normalizing FlowsLearn an invertible mapping between data and a simple distributionExact likelihood, stable training, invertible
The core promise of normalizing flows: they give you an exact probability for any data point, fast sampling, and the ability to go both ways between data space and a latent space.

Probability Basics (the essentials you need)

Before we dive in, let's build the minimal probability toolkit. No prior knowledge assumed.

What is a Probability Density Function?

A probability density function (PDF) tells us how likely different values are. Think of it like a landscape: tall hills mean the data clusters there, flat areas mean the data is sparse there.

Interactive: Explore a Gaussian (Normal) Distribution

Mean (mu): 0
Spread (sigma): 1

The area under this curve always equals 1. That is the fundamental rule of probability densities.

Key Intuition
The PDF is like a topographic map of probability. To find the probability of an event in a range, you measure the area under the curve in that range.

The Standard Normal: Our Starting Point

The standard normal distribution (mean = 0, standard deviation = 1) is written as \( \mathcal{N}(0, I) \). It is our workhorse because:

  • It is mathematically simple and well-understood
  • We can sample from it trivially
  • We can evaluate its density exactly
\[ p_Z(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{z^2}{2}\right) \]

Our entire goal will be: start with samples from this simple distribution, and push them through a transformation to get complex, realistic data.

2. The Key Idea: Transforming Distributions

Here is the central intuition behind normalizing flows. Suppose you have a simple base distribution (like a Gaussian), and you want to model a complex distribution (like the shape of a banana, or a face image).

What if you could learn a function \( f \) that warps the simple distribution into the complex one?

Base Distribution
\(p_Z(z)\)
Simple Gaussian
→ f (flow)
Data Distribution
\(p_X(x)\)
Complex data

The function \( f \) must satisfy two conditions:

  1. Invertible: we can go from \( z \) to \( x \), and also from \( x \) back to \( z \). This lets us both generate samples and evaluate probabilities.
  2. Differentiable: we need to be able to compute how volumes change under the transformation.

3. The Change of Variables Formula

Here is the mathematical heart of normalizing flows. When you transform a random variable through a function, how does the probability density change?

A 1D Analogy First

Suppose you have a random variable \( Z \) with density \( p_Z(z) \), and you define \( X = f(Z) \). What is the density of \( X \)?

Think physically: probability is like fluid. If you squeeze a pipe (compress space), the density goes up. If you expand it, the density goes down. The density must be rescaled by how much the transformation stretches or compresses space.

Visual: How Density Changes Under Transformation

Compression factor: 1.0x

When space is compressed (factor < 1), density increases. When it is stretched (factor > 1), density decreases. The total probability (area) stays exactly 1.

For a 1D transformation \( x = f(z) \), the change of variables formula is:

\[ p_X(x) = p_Z(f^{-1}(x)) \cdot \left|\frac{df^{-1}}{dx}\right| \]

Or equivalently, if we know \( f \) (the forward direction):

\[ p_X(x) = p_Z(z) \cdot \left|\frac{dz}{dx}\right| = \frac{p_Z(z)}{\left|\frac{dx}{dz}\right|} \]
Intuition
The term \(\left|\frac{dz}{dx}\right|\) tells you how much the transformation stretches or squishes space at point \(x\). You need this correction factor to make sure probabilities still sum to 1.

Extending to Multiple Dimensions: The Jacobian

Real data is multi-dimensional (images have millions of pixels!). In multiple dimensions, the "stretch factor" becomes a matrix called the Jacobian.

For a vector-valued function \( \mathbf{x} = f(\mathbf{z}) \) where both \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{z}\) are \(D\)-dimensional, the Jacobian matrix is:

\[ J_f = \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial \mathbf{z}} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial z_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial z_D} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial x_D}{\partial z_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial x_D}{\partial z_D} \end{pmatrix} \]

And the full change of variables formula in \(D\) dimensions is:

\[ \log p_X(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_Z(\mathbf{z}) - \log \left|\det J_f(\mathbf{z})\right| \]
The Big Challenge
Computing the determinant of a \(D \times D\) matrix costs \(\mathcal{O}(D^3)\). For a 64x64 image, \(D = 4096\). That is impossibly expensive. This is why the architectures in Section 4 are so clever: they design \(f\) so the Jacobian is triangular or diagonal, making the determinant trivially cheap.

Why the Log?

We work in log-space for numerical stability. Probabilities can be astronomically small, and multiplying them together causes underflow. Adding log-probabilities is stable and equivalent.

4. Building Blocks: Invertible Transformations

Now that we know the math, let's look at what kinds of transformations actually work in practice.

Requirements Recap

Must Have

  • Invertible (go both ways)
  • Differentiable (for training)
  • Tractable Jacobian determinant

Want to Have

  • Expressive (can model complex shapes)
  • Fast to compute in both directions
  • Parallelizable (for training speed)

Triangular Jacobians: The Key Trick

A matrix that is either upper or lower triangular has a determinant equal to the product of its diagonal entries. This turns an \(\mathcal{O}(D^3)\) computation into an \(\mathcal{O}(D)\) one.

\[ \det \begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & a_{13} \\ 0 & a_{22} & a_{23} \\ 0 & 0 & a_{33} \end{pmatrix} = a_{11} \cdot a_{22} \cdot a_{33} \]

Almost all practical flow architectures are designed so their Jacobian is triangular.

Affine Coupling Layers (RealNVP)

The most popular building block is the affine coupling layer. Here is exactly how it works, step by step.

Core Idea
Split the input into two halves. Transform one half using a neural network that depends on the other half. The other half passes through unchanged. This structure guarantees invertibility while allowing the network to be arbitrarily complex.

Forward Pass (generating data from latent)

  1. Split: divide the \(D\)-dimensional input \(\mathbf{z}\) into two parts: \(\mathbf{z}_{1:d}\) and \(\mathbf{z}_{d+1:D}\).
  2. Pass through unchanged: \(\mathbf{x}_{1:d} = \mathbf{z}_{1:d}\). The first half is copied directly.
  3. Compute scale and shift: feed \(\mathbf{z}_{1:d}\) into a neural network to get \(s = \text{NN}_s(\mathbf{z}_{1:d})\) and \(t = \text{NN}_t(\mathbf{z}_{1:d})\).
  4. Transform second half: \(\mathbf{x}_{d+1:D} = \mathbf{z}_{d+1:D} \odot \exp(s) + t\). The \(\odot\) means element-wise multiplication.
\[ \mathbf{x}_{1:d} = \mathbf{z}_{1:d} \qquad \mathbf{x}_{d+1:D} = \mathbf{z}_{d+1:D} \odot \exp\bigl(s(\mathbf{z}_{1:d})\bigr) + t(\mathbf{z}_{1:d}) \]

Inverse Pass (encoding data to latent)

The inverse is simple because the first half is unchanged:

\[ \mathbf{z}_{1:d} = \mathbf{x}_{1:d} \qquad \mathbf{z}_{d+1:D} = \bigl(\mathbf{x}_{d+1:D} - t(\mathbf{x}_{1:d})\bigr) \odot \exp\bigl(-s(\mathbf{x}_{1:d})\bigr) \]

Jacobian of a Coupling Layer

Because the first half is untouched, the Jacobian is lower triangular:

\[ J = \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial \mathbf{z}} = \begin{pmatrix} I_d & 0 \\ \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}_{d+1:D}}{\partial \mathbf{z}_{1:d}} & \text{diag}(\exp(s)) \end{pmatrix} \]

The determinant is just the product of the diagonal of the lower-right block:

\[ \log \left|\det J\right| = \sum_{i=d+1}^{D} s_i(\mathbf{z}_{1:d}) \]

Watch how the top half passes through unchanged while the bottom half gets scaled and shifted based on the top half.

Why Do We Alternate Which Half is Transformed?

In a single coupling layer, the first half never changes. To make both halves expressive, we alternate: even layers transform \(\mathbf{z}_{d+1:D}\) based on \(\mathbf{z}_{1:d}\), and odd layers do the opposite. After just 2 layers, every dimension has been transformed at least once.

5. Training Normalizing Flows

Training is done by maximum likelihood estimation: we want the model to assign high probability to the data we observe.

The Training Objective

Given a dataset \(\{x^{(1)}, \ldots, x^{(N)}\}\), we maximize the log-likelihood:

\[ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log p_\theta\bigl(\mathbf{x}^{(i)}\bigr) \]

Using the change of variables formula, this expands to:

\[ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[ \log p_Z\bigl(f_\theta^{-1}(\mathbf{x}^{(i)})\bigr) + \log \left|\det J_{f_\theta^{-1}}\bigl(\mathbf{x}^{(i)}\bigr)\right| \right] \]

For a flow with \(K\) stacked transformations:

\[ \log p_X(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_Z(\mathbf{z}_0) + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \log \left|\det J_{f_k^{-1}}(\mathbf{z}_k)\right| \]
Plain English
To train the model, we encode each data point into the latent space using the inverse flow. The model is good when (1) encoded points look like samples from a Gaussian, AND (2) the transformations do not distort volumes too much. Both terms are in the loss.

The Training Loop (Conceptually)

  1. Sample a batch of real data points \(\{\mathbf{x}^{(i)}\}\)
  2. Encode: pass each \(\mathbf{x}^{(i)}\) through the inverse flow \(f^{-1}\) to get \(\mathbf{z}^{(i)}\), accumulating the log-determinant sum along the way
  3. Evaluate: compute \(\log p_Z(\mathbf{z}^{(i)})\) under the Gaussian base distribution
  4. Loss: the negative average log-likelihood (we minimize, so we negate the quantity we want to maximize)
  5. Backpropagate and update model parameters

Sampling from a Trained Flow

Once trained, generating new data is simple:

  1. Sample \(\mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\) (trivial)
  2. Pass through forward flow: \(\mathbf{x} = f(\mathbf{z})\)
  3. \(\mathbf{x}\) is a new realistic data sample

6. Worked Example: 2D Moons Dataset

Let us trace through a complete, concrete example. Our goal: learn to generate points that look like two interleaved half-moons.

Step-by-Step Flow Transformation Demo

The Data Distribution

The two-moons dataset lives in 2D. The data is complex: it has two curved clusters, and any good generative model needs to capture this multi-modal structure. A single Gaussian would completely fail here.

\[ \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2, \quad \mathbf{x} \sim p_{\text{data}} \]

Encoding Data to Latent Space

We pass real data through the inverse flow. After training, the encoded points \(\mathbf{z} = f^{-1}(\mathbf{x})\) should look like a standard Gaussian. This is the "normalizing" part: complex data is normalized into a simple distribution.

\[ \mathbf{z} = f^{-1}(\mathbf{x}), \quad \text{want } \mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I) \]

Computing the Loss

For each encoded point, we compute two quantities and sum them:

1. How well the encoded points match a Gaussian: \(\log p_Z(\mathbf{z})\)

2. The log-determinant correction for volume change: \(\log |\det J|\)

\[ \text{loss} = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_i \left[ \log p_Z(\mathbf{z}_i) + \log |\det J_i| \right] \]

Generating New Samples

After training, to generate a new moon-shaped point, we sample \(\mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\) and pass it forward through the trained flow. The result looks like it came from the original moons dataset.

\[ \mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I) \implies \mathbf{x} = f(\mathbf{z}) \sim p_{\text{data}} \]

What the Flow Learns

The flow learns a series of smooth, invertible deformations that morph the circular Gaussian into the two-moon shape. Think of it like bending and twisting a sheet of rubber: every point moves, but the sheet never tears or overlaps itself (invertibility).

Latent Space

Circular Gaussian blob - symmetric, simple, easy to sample from

Data Space

Two curved, interleaved moons - complex, multi-modal structure

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class MLP(nn.Module):
    """Simple multilayer perceptron used inside coupling layers."""
    def __init__(self, in_dim, out_dim, hidden=64):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(in_dim, hidden), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, out_dim)
        )
    def forward(self, x):
        return self.net(x)


class AffineCouplingLayer(nn.Module):
    """
    RealNVP-style affine coupling.
    Splits input in half: first half passes unchanged,
    second half gets scaled and shifted by an MLP applied to the first half.
    """
    def __init__(self, dim, hidden=64):
        super().__init__()
        self.d = dim // 2
        # Scale network (outputs log-scale for stability)
        self.s_net = MLP(self.d, dim - self.d, hidden)
        # Shift network
        self.t_net = MLP(self.d, dim - self.d, hidden)

    def forward(self, z):
        z1, z2 = z[:, :self.d], z[:, self.d:]
        s = self.s_net(z1)         # log-scale
        t = self.t_net(z1)         # shift
        x1 = z1
        x2 = z2 * torch.exp(s) + t
        x = torch.cat([x1, x2], dim=1)
        log_det = s.sum(dim=1)     # sum over features
        return x, log_det

    def inverse(self, x):
        x1, x2 = x[:, :self.d], x[:, self.d:]
        s = self.s_net(x1)
        t = self.t_net(x1)
        z1 = x1
        z2 = (x2 - t) * torch.exp(-s)
        z = torch.cat([z1, z2], dim=1)
        log_det = -s.sum(dim=1)    # negative for inverse
        return z, log_det


class NormalizingFlow(nn.Module):
    """
    A stack of affine coupling layers with alternating splits.
    After each layer we flip the split so both halves get transformed.
    """
    def __init__(self, dim=2, n_layers=8, hidden=64):
        super().__init__()
        self.layers = nn.ModuleList(
            [AffineCouplingLayer(dim, hidden) for _ in range(n_layers)]
        )
        # Permutation indices: alternate which half is transformed
        self.perms = [
            torch.arange(dim) if i % 2 == 0
            else torch.cat([torch.arange(dim // 2, dim),
                            torch.arange(dim // 2)])
            for i in range(n_layers)
        ]

    def log_prob(self, x):
        """Compute exact log-likelihood of data under the model."""
        z = x
        total_log_det = torch.zeros(x.shape[0], device=x.device)
        for layer, perm in zip(self.layers, self.perms):
            z = z[:, perm]
            z, log_det = layer.inverse(z)
            total_log_det += log_det
        # Base distribution: standard Gaussian
        log_pz = -0.5 * (z ** 2 + torch.log(torch.tensor(2 * 3.14159))).sum(1)
        return log_pz + total_log_det

    @torch.no_grad()
    def sample(self, n):
        """Generate n samples by running the forward flow."""
        z = torch.randn(n, 2)
        for layer, perm in zip(reversed(self.layers),
                               reversed(self.perms)):
            z, _ = layer(z)
            inv_perm = torch.argsort(perm)
            z = z[:, inv_perm]
        return z
python train.py - Training Loop
import torch
import numpy as np
from sklearn.datasets import make_moons

def get_moons_data(n=2000, noise=0.05):
    X, _ = make_moons(n_samples=n, noise=noise, random_state=42)
    return torch.tensor(X, dtype=torch.float32)

def train(model, data, epochs=2000, lr=1e-3, batch_size=512):
    optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=lr)
    scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.CosineAnnealingLR(
        optimizer, T_max=epochs
    )
    losses = []
    for epoch in range(epochs):
        # Random mini-batch
        idx = torch.randperm(len(data))[:batch_size]
        x_batch = data[idx]

        # Loss = negative mean log-likelihood
        loss = -model.log_prob(x_batch).mean()

        optimizer.zero_grad()
        loss.backward()
        torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), 1.0)
        optimizer.step()
        scheduler.step()

        losses.append(loss.item())
        if (epoch + 1) % 200 == 0:
            print(f"Epoch {epoch+1:4d} | Loss: {loss.item():.4f}")
    return losses

# Run training
data = get_moons_data(n=3000)
model = NormalizingFlow(dim=2, n_layers=8, hidden=128)
losses = train(model, data, epochs=2000)

"""
Normalizing Flow on Two-Moons Dataset
Run: python full_flow.py
"""
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.datasets import make_moons

# ---- Model ----

class MLP(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, in_d, out_d, h=64):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(in_d, h), nn.Tanh(),
            nn.Linear(h, h), nn.Tanh(),
            nn.Linear(h, out_d)
        )
    def forward(self, x): return self.net(x)

class CouplingLayer(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, dim, hidden=64):
        super().__init__()
        d = dim // 2
        self.d = d
        self.s = MLP(d, dim - d, hidden)
        self.t = MLP(d, dim - d, hidden)

    def forward(self, z):
        z1, z2 = z[:, :self.d], z[:, self.d:]
        s, t = self.s(z1), self.t(z1)
        return torch.cat([z1, z2 * s.exp() + t], 1), s.sum(1)

    def inverse(self, x):
        x1, x2 = x[:, :self.d], x[:, self.d:]
        s, t = self.s(x1), self.t(x1)
        return torch.cat([x1, (x2 - t) * (-s).exp()], 1), -s.sum(1)

class Flow(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, dim=2, n=8, h=128):
        super().__init__()
        self.layers = nn.ModuleList([CouplingLayer(dim, h) for _ in range(n)])
        d = dim // 2
        self.perms = [
            torch.arange(dim) if i % 2 == 0
            else torch.cat([torch.arange(d, dim), torch.arange(d)])
            for i in range(n)
        ]

    def log_prob(self, x):
        z, ldj = x, torch.zeros(len(x))
        for layer, p in zip(self.layers, self.perms):
            z = z[:, p]
            z, ld = layer.inverse(z)
            ldj += ld
        log_pz = -0.5 * (z**2 + torch.log(2*torch.tensor(torch.pi))).sum(1)
        return log_pz + ldj

    @torch.no_grad()
    def sample(self, n):
        z = torch.randn(n, 2)
        for layer, p in zip(reversed(self.layers), reversed(self.perms)):
            z, _ = layer(z)
            z = z[:, torch.argsort(p)]
        return z

9. Key Intuitions and Summary

The Three Core Ideas

  1. Simple to complex: Start with a Gaussian, apply a learned invertible function, get complex data. The function warps probability mass without creating or destroying it.
  2. Volume correction: When you stretch or squeeze space, density must compensate. The Jacobian determinant tracks this accounting precisely.
  3. Triangular structure: Designing the Jacobian to be triangular makes the otherwise impossible \(\mathcal{O}(D^3)\) determinant computation cheap at \(\mathcal{O}(D)\).

Advantages of Normalizing Flows

  • Exact log-likelihood computation
  • Stable training (no adversarial game)
  • Invertible encoding and decoding
  • Principled uncertainty quantification
  • Useful for density estimation, anomaly detection
  • Can condition on labels or other data

Limitations

  • The invertibility constraint limits the types of architectures you can use
  • Often require more parameters than GANs for similar sample quality
  • Scaling to very high-dimensional data (e.g., large images) is expensive
  • Recent diffusion models have surpassed flows in raw image quality

Where Flows Are Still Best in Class

  • Scientific applications: molecular conformations, physics simulations, where exact likelihoods matter
  • Anomaly detection: unusual samples get low probability scores
  • Uncertainty estimation: you can quantify confidence by evaluating the density
  • Latent variable models: the encoding is lossless and deterministic
Further Reading
Key papers: NICE (Dinh 2014), RealNVP (Dinh 2016), Glow (Kingma 2018), MAF (Papamakarios 2017), "Normalizing Flows for Probabilistic Modeling and Inference" survey (Papamakarios 2021).
